Hedy Lamarr

Hollywood actress and the woman who invented Wi-Fi

Hedy Lamarr, pictured here at the height of her fame.

Here is a timeline of Hedy's life

  • 1914 Born Hedwig Eva Maria Kiesler on November 9th.
  • 1928 In the late 20's Lamarr was discovered as an actress by producer Max Reinhardt.
  • 1933 At age 18, she starred in Gustav Machatý's film, Ecstasy.
  • 1933 Later that year Lamarr married Friedrich Mandl, the first of 6 marriages.
  • 1937 She divorced Mandl and went to Paris where she met Louis B. Mayer.
  • 1938 She moved to Hollywood and made her American debut in "Algiers".
  • 1940 Lead roles in "Comrade X" with Clark Gable and "Boom Town", again with Gable and Spencer Tracy.
  • 1942 With the help of her friend, the composer George Antheil, she patented plans for a device that could produce a frequency-hopping signal that could not be tracked or jammed, this could block radio controlled torpedos and send them off course. However the US military was not interested.
  • 1949 Despite a very lonely personal lif she had 2 children and made 18 films between 1940-49.
  • 1953 Lamarr became a naturalized citizen of the United States at age 38
  • 1962 An updated version of her radio signal device appear on US battle ships during the cuban missile crisis. Lamarr and Antheil's work with spread spectrum technology led to the development of GPS, Bluetooth, and Wi-Fi.
  • 1966 Her autobiography, Ecstasy and Me, was published.
  • 1974 She filed a $10 million lawsuit against Warner Bros., claiming that the running parody of her name ("Hedley Lamarr") in the Mel Brooks comedy Blazing Saddles infringed her right to privacy.
  • 1997 Hedy received the Electronic Frontier Foundation Pioneer Award and the Bulbie Gnass Spirit of Achievement Bronze Award, for individuals whose creative lifetime achievements in the arts, sciences, business, or invention fields have significantly contributed to society.
  • 2000 Lamarr died in Altamonte Springs, Florida on January 19, 2000, aged 85.
  • 2014 Lamarr and Antheil were posthumously inducted into the National Inventors Hall of Fame.
